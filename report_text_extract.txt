ABSTRACT 
This paper presents the Engineering Management AI Agentic System (eMAS), 
a full-stack manufacturing management prototype designed to support factory 
operations through natural-language interaction, intelligent decision support, 
and workflow automation. Traditional engineering management systems often 
require users to navigate multiple menus, manually search operational data, 
and prepare schedules or reports with high effort. To address this issue, eMAS 
integrates a React-based frontend, a Go/Gin backend API, a MySQL database, 
and a Python FastAPI Factory Agent that connects user requests to backend 
tools through an agent workflow. 
The system supports common manufacturing management tasks such as job 
scheduling, production data access, reporting, dashboard visualisation, 
machine and inventory queries, material shortage handling, and approval-
controlled operational actions. Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is also 
used to support document-based advisory responses where relevant. The 
system was evaluated using simulated factory data, seeded manufacturing 
scenarios, automated test results, system logs, end-to-end workflow evidence, 
and a task-based usability survey. 
The evaluation results show that eMAS can reduce manual interaction, 
improve access to operational information, and provide safer AI-assisted 
decision support in a smart manufacturing environment. The final executed 
frontend unit/component and Playwright end-to-end verification gates recorded 
0 failed tests, while the user survey achieved an overall mean score of 4.71 
out of 5.00. These findings indicate that eMAS is a functional and usable 
prototype for AI-assisted manufacturing management, although further 
validation with live factory data and real industrial users is recommended for 
future work.

Chapter 1 Introduction  
1.1 Background of Research  
Today's manufacturing sectors are growing increasingly competitive and 
complex. For modern factories to remain productive, processes must be 
straightforward, and digital technologies must be highly accurate. The 
Industrie 4.0 Final Report states that information and communication 
technology (ICT) already supports around 90% of industrial manufacturing 
processes in Germany, demonstrating the significance of digital systems for 
manufacturing (Kagermann et al., 2013). 
 
Furthermore, production is also becoming more automated and connected. 
Industry 4.0 is based on smart networking between machines, systems, and 
products, using tools like the embedded systems, Internet of Things, and data 
sync (Kagermann et al., 2013). These technologies help machines work 
together, communicate, and even make some decisions on their own. 
Research shows that Industry 4.0 supports flexible and self-managing 
production, which improves efficiency and makes it easier to create 
customized products (Kagermann et al., 2013). 
 
Although major businesses have been quick to embrace Industry 4.0, most of 
the small and medium-sized businesses (SMEs) are yet to embrace advanced 
digital management systems because of the expense, complexity of the 
system, and high technical expertise. 
 
Consequently, there is increased need to have lightweight and friendly to use 
and intelligent factory management devices that do not necessitate some high 
levels of technical expertise to carry out the whole process of making decisions. 
 


--- PAGE 14 ---
 
2 
 
With the ongoing development of factories, there is an increasing need to have 
systems that will be able to unify data, disseminate information effectively, and 
assist in making timely decisions. This movement has also increased the rate 
of artificial intelligence (AI) technology adoption, specifically, AI agents, as a 
way of increasing the smarts of systems and their usability in manufacturing 
settings. 
 
In recent years, Artificial Intelligence (AI) has become a crucial tool for making 
work systems better. AI agents are computer programs that can notice what 
is happening, learn from information, and respond to different situations. They 
help people by giving quick access to useful details and by taking over 
repetitive tasks. AI also has been solving problems by planning the steps 
needed to reach a goal for a long time. These systems observe the situation, 
understand the possible actions, and choose the best way to solve the 
problem. In manufacturing, this is helpful for tasks that consist of a lot of steps 
automatically. Since the 1970s, industry has employed this type of AI. The 
structure of intelligent agents, including simple ones that just react to what 
they sense and more advanced ones that remember information, helps design 
machine programs so machines can work safely and effectively in different 
situations (Russell, Stuart & Norvig, 2021).  
 
Many traditional engineering management systems still require users to set up 
schedules by hand, click through multiple menus, and read complex data. This 
makes daily work slower and increases the chances of mistakes. 
 
1.2 Problem Statement  
Research shows that smart agents are becoming increasingly vital in 
manufacturing because they assist machines in working smarter and react 
faster. For example, an agent can notice if a tool is wearing out and take the 
right action (Park & Tran, 2017). They can communicate with other machines, 
operate independently, adjust to changing conditions, and assist in problem-


--- PAGE 15 ---
 
3 
 
solving. In essence, they perceive, reason, and behave somewhat like people 
do when making choices. 
 
However, many factories still do not use these intelligent systems. The 
commercial manufacturing execution and planning systems currently in place 
are considered very expensive to use, difficult to set up and demand technical 
expertise to maintain. This renders them inappropriate or unfeasible to the 
SMEs, which usually have relatively small budgets and less technical staff. 
One reason is that they are difficult to integrate, lack standardization, and can 
be complicated to manage (Pulikottil et al., 2023). At the same time, modern 
manufacturing requires systems that can work autonomously, react in real 
time, share information smoothly, and adapt quickly when something changes. 
Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) are known to be helpful for tasks like planning, 
scheduling, monitoring, and handling unexpected problems (Pulikottil et al., 
2023). But many factories still use old, centralized systems that are rigid and 
not flexible enough to support intelligent decision taking  (Pulikottil et al., 2023). 
 
Because of these issues, there is a gap between what modern factories need 
and what current systems can do. Workers still must manually schedule jobs, 
look for data, and make reports, which take time and can lead to mistakes.  
 
1.3 Research Purpose  
This study aims to show how an agentic system powered by AI can enhance 
engineering management within a smart manufacturing setting through the 
obligation to change the normal operational controlling mode of the interface 
into intelligent, conversational, and autonomous assistance. 
 
This paper aims to create a viable vision of how artificial intelligence can be 
applied in the management of a factory in a manner that can improve human 
possibilities and not to substitute them. The study will help achieve more

In the end, the work hopes to demonstrate how agentic AI systems can

3. To automate selected factory-management workflows while keeping

4. To provide decision support for scheduling, shortages, machine and

5. To evaluate eMAS using simulated factory data, task-based usability 
measures, system logs, and automated test scenarios.

production-data access compared with menu-based workflows? 
3. How useful are the AI assistant’s recommendations for scheduling, 
shortage handling, and operational decision support? 
4. Does the approval workflow help keep automated business-changing 
actions safe and traceable?

1.6 Project Scope  
The scope of this project covers the design, implementation, and evaluation 
of the Engineering Management AI Agentic System (eMAS) as a full-stack 
manufacturing management prototype. The system includes a React-based 
frontend interface, a Go/Gin backend API, a MySQL database, and a Python 
FastAPI Factory Agent that allows users to interact with the system through 
natural-language requests. The prototype supports factory-management 
functions such as job scheduling, production data viewing, dashboard 
visualization, reporting, inventory and machine-resource queries, shortage 
analysis, and AI-assisted recommendations. The Factory Agent is connected 
to backend tools and can assist users by retrieving operational data, preparing 
responses, and supporting selected workflow actions. For actions that may 
change business data, the system applies a human approval process before 
execution. 
 
This project is limited to a simulated or sample-data manufacturing 
environment and does not involve direct control of physical machines, real-
time sensor integration, or deployment in a live industrial factory. The purpose 
is to evaluate whether an AI-assisted engineering management prototype can 
reduce manual interaction, improve access to factory information, and support 
better decision-making in representative manufacturing scenarios. 
 
1.7 Significance of the Research  
This study is important because it focuses on the issues that remain 
unresolved by the contemporary factories whose management systems still 
are manual and intricate. The study illustrates that AI agents can ease the 
factory management systems, decrease the operational delays, and enhance

to communicate with complex systems through simple, human readable 
commands as opposed to special software interfaces. This is especially useful

To the factory workers and managers, eMAS lowers manual repetitive tasks

understand and view. In the case of the manufacturing business, the study

research work gives an insight into the real-world implementation of AI agentic 
systems and natural language interfaces in smart factory settings and can be 
used in the future of Industry 4.0 intelligent manufacturing systems.

2.2 Industry 4.0 
Industry 4.0 is reforming the operational patterns of the factories through the 
employment of digital technologies, linked machines, and intelligent 
automation. The previous factories were centralized and employed hand labor, 
thus making the process slow and less adaptable. Smart factories, in their turn, 
make decisions automatically using real-time data and interconnected 
machines. This makes production quicker, more efficient, and more adaptable. 
The study of Industry 4.0 will be significant to the development of eMAS since 
these tools will generate the environment in which intelligent agents will be 
able to assist in performing such duties as scheduling, monitoring, and 
reporting. 
 
2.2.1 Overview of Industry 4.0 
Industry 4.0 transforms previous factories into more intelligent and 
interconnected working places. Contrary to the conventional factories where 
people worked by hand and decisions were made centrally, factories adopt 
real-time information and machines that can make some decisions even 
independently. This simplifies production, increases its flexibility, and can take 
custom orders (Lu, 2017). 
 
Using these technologies can really boost factory performance. For instance, 
a study by Büchi et al. (2020) looked at 231 factories and found that both the 
number of technologies a factory uses and how well they are connected affect 
its success. Factories that fully embrace these technologies tend to see bigger 
improvements. 
 
Even smaller factories benefit. In Italy, firms that adopted several methods find 
their labor productivity rise by about 7% measure against those that did not 
(Bettiol et al., 2024). A review by Kim et al. (2023) also indicated that smart 
manufacturing does not only make profit and cost reduction easier but also 
helps in innovation, flexibility, and employee satisfaction. 


--- PAGE 21 ---
 
9 
 
 
The change is driven by technologies like machines, sensors, and devices 
linked via the Internet of Things, which share data instantly and make 
monitoring and decision-making faster (Lu, 2017). Cyber-physical systems link 
machines with computers, letting them sense, think, and act while working 
together with other machines. Research by Oks et al. (2022) shows these 
systems have many uses in smart factories. Cloud computing and cloud 
manufacturing store and process machine data, helping factories plan better, 
predict problems, and share information across locations (Shiyong Wang et 
al., 2016). 
 
2.2.2 Connectivity and Interoperability 
In Industry 4.0, connectivity and interoperability are especially important 
because they let machines, systems, and distinct parts of a factory share data 
in real time. During interconnection, machines, sensors, and devices can 
transmit and receive information amazingly fast. This assists the automation 
to respond quicker, adjust production dynamically, and organize activities even 
more effectively. 
 
Real-time information is also important in control and decision-making. Here, 
an example is cyber-physical system (CPS), which involves live sensor data 
to identify issues and act upon them in real time (Oks et al., 2024). With this 
connectivity, the smart factories are in a position to automate more than a 
single machine. They can fully control the entire process of the entire factory.  
 
Interoperability implies that various systems can be interoperable and 
communicate with each other. Interoperability allows one machine or program 
to access data in another machine or program without additional effort. This 
prevents replication of information that is trapped in different data silos and 
prevents repetition of work that has been done. A major issue with Industry 4.0 
is that not all devices adhere to the same rule; thus, they will not necessarily 


--- PAGE 22 ---
 
10 
 
be able to communicate with one another, and completing the interoperability 
becomes difficult (Lu, 2017). 
 
Conventional manufacturing systems are also challenged with this. Aging 
machines also tend to have their own communication protocols, meaning they 
are not easily integrated into the current systems. In the absence of standard 
rules, it is hard to incorporate old machines into a real-time network. 
 
Another challenge is managing real-time data. Collecting, sending, and 
processing data from many sensors all at once can put a heavy load on 
systems. Even CPS can struggle with fast data rates, network delays, and 
keeping everything synchronized when real-time control is needed (Oks et al., 
2024). 
 
Achieving true interoperability requires more than technology. It also depends 
on organization and shared standards. For example, a review by Acharya et 
al. (2024) found that embedding digital twins within cyber-physical systems 
needs multiple levels of interoperability, including technical, syntactic, 
semantic, and organizational aspects. The study pointed out at least seventy-
seven challenges, such as making sure systems understand the meaning of 
shared data and aligning processes across departments. This shows why full 
interoperability is complex and not easy to achieve. 
 
2.2.3 Challenges in Modern Manufacturing 
The current manufacturing industry is still challenged by the substantial 
number of problems that change with the transition to Industry 4.0  as factories 
shift to more advanced methods, the needs of the organization evolve, and 
operation should be based on data. 
 


--- PAGE 23 ---
 
11 
 
Navigating complexity and executive decision processes becomes more 
challenging with the increase in the globalization of factories, manufacturers 
are forced to deal with increased volumes of data, more sophisticated 
machines, and improved inter-process coordination. This also brings in 
additional levels of decision-making that the conventional systems did not 
encounter. Studies show that handling systems successfully requires 
structured decision-support tools because of the higher volume of data and 
the need for more coordination (Fatorachian & Kazemi, 2018). 
 
Big data projects and quality 4.0 are also challenging because its initiatives 
aim to improve quality and prediction in manufacturing. But most such projects 
do not generate long-term value. In a review, 80 to 87 percent of 
manufacturing big-data undertakings fail to survive due to lack of clarity and 
preparation in the organizations (Escobar et al., 2021). 
 
Overseeing connectivity and infrastructure integration is also a problem 
because connecting old machines with modern solutions continues as a 
problem. Different private communication in old machines also results in 
fragmentation of systems and interfaces that cannot be compatible. Several 
types of hardware make it harder to connect everything together. These 
interoperability problems complicate the work of the manufacturers to design 
a seamless and high-performing digital system. As an example, Alqoud et al. 
(2022) have found that when attaching IoT sensors and gateways to older 
machines, there are serious problems with the different protocols, and there 
are no standard communication rules that make integration even harder. 
 
2.2.4 Concept of Intelligent Agents 
Intelligent agents refer to computer code that is capable of detecting what is 
occurring in its environment, reasoning about it, and performing a response. 
This is commonly referred to as the perceive, reason, and act cycle 


--- PAGE 24 ---
 
12 
 
(Wooldridge & Jennings, 1995). Other agents are reactive, i.e., they act now 
they feel without thinking. Others are more complex, known as deliberative 
agents, in the sense that they are able to anticipate actions to be taken in the 
future according to the possibilities. 
 
All these agents can be designed in diverse ways. The BDI model, which can 
be defined as Belief, Desire, and Intention, is one of them. It assists an agent 
to discern the world (beliefs), make decisions on what it desires to reach 
(desires), and plan on what to do next (intentions) (Georgeff & Rao, 1995). 
The other method is hybrid or layered architecture. In them, the agents are 
structured in levels. The upper levels are involved in high-level decisions, and 
the lower ones are involved with routine activities (Wooldridge and Jennings, 
1995). 
 
Intelligent agents come in handy in manufacturing and Industry 4.0. They are 
able to track the machines, plan production, and identify issues as well as 
collaborate with other agents. It was found that agent utilization can help make 
factories quicker and more efficient, particularly when decisions are required 
in a brief time and the conditions are constantly altered (Pulikottil et al., 2023). 
 
2.2.4.1 BDI-Based Reasoning for Manufacturing Scheduling in Industry 
4.0 
Belief-Desire-Intention (BDI) model is a structured and systematic way of 
reasoning that is suitable in manufacturing scheduling in the environment. 
Beliefs in this context reflect the current perception of an agent of the machine 
state of the factory, such as its machine availability, job status, order priority 
and faults detected, constantly updated by cyber-physical systems and sensor 
data. According to these beliefs, the agents develop desires that mirror 
production goals like reducing job lateness, maximizing machine use and 
urgency of orders. The agent then makes commitments that are the actions of 
concrete scheduling, such as reassigning jobs, or changing a sequence of 
production or rescheduling activities or tasks in response to disruptions in the 


--- PAGE 25 ---
 
13 
 
operations. Recent research indicates that the BDI-based reasoning is a good 
strategy to use in decision making in dynamic and uncertain situations. 
Karaduman et al. (2026) demonstrate that improved agents of BDI like fuzzy-
BDI architectures can adapt their reasoning in intelligent cyber-physical 
systems through dealing with uncertainty of real-time data. Moreover, such 
formal modelling and verification of BDI agents in a state of uncertainty also 
increase their reliability and quality of correctness in the application of real-
time industrial operations (Archibald et al., 2025) These results justify the use 
of BDI-based agents as fundamental rationale in building systems that are 
intelligent scheduling and management, like theai agentic Engineering 
Management AI Agentic System (eMAS). 
2.2.5 Multi-Agent Systems in Manufacturing 
Multi-agent systems (MAS) refer to those systems in which multiple intelligent 
agents are incorporated, which may act independently and operate collectively 
in achieving common goals. Applications of MAS in manufacturing have been 
witnessed to make distributed decisions, autonomous scheduling, and 
automatic machine interaction. These systems can be effectively applied, 
particularly in intelligent factories whereby the production processes must be 
dynamic with fluctuations of demand or availability of machines or any other 
dynamic factors.  
 
Recent studies have solid evidence concerning the effectiveness of MAS. Li 
et al. (2025) have developed a MAS that is built on deep reinforcement 
learning (DRL) in self-organizing resource-based scheduling. This system was 
much better than the old schedule regulations and was responsive to dynamic 
changes and also was more efficient in production. Similarly, in a work on 
decentralized MAS to schedule machine shops online, the agents found out 
that they could learn how to place bids on work and decide when to initiate a 
job, resulting in reductions of job tardiness of 25 percent compared to 
traditional scheduling algorithms (Didden et al., 2023). 
 


--- PAGE 26 ---
 
14 
 
MAS using multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) has shown itself to 
assist in the process of incrementing online layout planning in the flex system 
and scheduling during digital production planning and timetabling. The 
strategy enabled assembly lines to be reconfigured within a brief time in 
response to order type or demand variation to boost the flexibility of the entire 
system (Kaven et al., 2024). The machines and robots are also assisted by 
MAS in coordination. Making quality solutions with less computation time than 
the centralized scheduling which improves efficiency of the operations, the 
multi-agent approach to retrieving orders and robot task plans control in 
robotic mobile fulfillment systems (RMFS) has given good solutions. (Teck et 
al., 2023).  
 
Overall, these findings indicate that MAS will be in a position to achieve such 
goals as distributed decision-making, real-time responsiveness, flexibility, and 
superior scheduling performance. Regarding systems like eMAS, autonomous 
scheduling, better coordination, and a more responsive production process 
can be achieved because of adopting MAS architecture. 
2.2.6 Benefits and Challenges of MAS 
A lot of advantages that the contemporary smart factories have compared to 
MAS are numerous, but among the largest ones, there exists flexibility. It is 
able to quickly adjust to any changes in the event of a machine failure or 
sudden peak in demand. Studies have also shown that factory plants are more 
able to handle complex and unforeseeable conditions using MARL 
(Bahrpeyma & Reichelt, 2022). 
MAS also facilitates autonomy, which allows for decentralized decision-
making among agents: based on their knowledge, individual agents are able 
to make decisions locally, without being supervised by an external central 
control. For gaining insights, the partially decentralized scheduling in a self-
organizing MAS was much more adaptive with respect to dynamic 
disturbances (new order arrival; machine failure) than classical scheduling 
approaches (Li et al., 2025). 


--- PAGE 27 ---
 
15 
 
Another strength is scalability. With an increase in the number of factories, 
machines, workstations, or production lines, MAS architecture enables the 
addition of new agents without modifying the entire control system. A review 
of studies on smart-manufacturing networks indicates that MAS has good 
scaling capability for growing or reconfigurable production lines (Guo et al., 
2023). 
The protocol used for one stated implementation of MAS is another issue. 
Existing MAS protocols differ greatly in terms of rules and guidelines, making 
it very difficult to integrate them or standardize them. This was highlighted in 
a recent review on agent-based manufacturing, where the lack of industry 
standards and difficulties in incorporating MAS into older legacy systems 
remain stubborn implementation barriers (Pulikottil et al., 2023). 
Second, MAS is not widely implemented due to complexity and integration 
overhead. A large capital investment is required to ensure proper integration 
throughout the manufacturing process to support decentralized control. 
Moreover, the lack of awareness and the need for experts to apply these 
complicated systems still prevents their extensive use in industry (Pulikottil et 
al., 2023). 
Finally, stability and coordination can be an issue. While a few agents may 
coordinate easily, conflicts or oscillations can occur when many agents are 
involved unless they are strategically designed. Reviews have shown that 
MAS encounters difficulty maintaining stable and consistent behaviour under 
high concurrency of agents in manufacturing (Bahrpeyma & Reichelt, 2022).

2.3.1 NLP for Industrial Applications  
Getting familiar with factory terminology, ensuring that NLP performs fast in 
noisy conditions, and integrating it with the existing systems require specific

Chatbots are one of the applications, which serve as virtual assistants to

NLP also has been used to support decision-support systems with

schedules, and identifies patterns on the surface to increase the efficiency of

2.3.3 AI-Based Decision Support Systems 
AI based decision support systems (DSS) are becoming more common in 
modern factories because they help managers make better and faster 
decisions. Instead of showing only fixed charts or static dashboards, AI 
systems can study data in real time and give suggestions based on machine 
learning and prediction models. A recent study explains that AI based DSS can 
support factories by improving planning, quality control, and machine 
monitoring (Soori et al., 2024). 
AI DSS can also help with predictive maintenance. The system checks sensor 
data from machines and learns what normal behaviour looks like. When it sees 
something unusual, it warns operators early so they can fix the issue before 
the machine fails. This helps reduce downtime and improves reliability (Soori 
et al., 2024). 
Another use of AI DSS is in quality monitoring. Research shows that machine 
learning can find small changes or errors in production that humans may miss. 
By detecting defects early, factories can reduce waste and improve product 
quality (Lin & Chen, 2024).

19 
 
AI DSS also supports production planning. Instead of using fixed rules, the 
system studies the current demand, machine availability, and material supply. 
It can then recommend better production schedules or adjustments to meet 
new conditions. This makes the factory more flexible and able to react to 
change (Soori et al., 2024). 
Even with these benefits, AI DSS has some limits. It needs high quality data, 
good sensors, and systems that can work together. It also requires skilled 
workers who understand how to use AI recommendations safely. Without

Overall, AI based DSS offer a clearer and more useful way to support

act earlier, understand problems faster, and run production more smoothly. 
 
2.3.4 LLM-Based Agentic Systems and Tool Use 
 
Recent developments in Large Language Models (LLMs) have expanded the 
role of AI systems from simple text generation to agentic systems that can 
reason, plan, and interact with external tools. LLM-based agents are designed 
to receive user instructions, break down tasks, select suitable actions, and use 
available resources to complete the task. Wang et al. (2024) explain that LLM-
based autonomous agents normally include components such as planning, 
memory, tool use, and action execution. This is important for eMAS because 
the system does not only provide a chatbot response, but also connects 
natural-language requests to backend manufacturing tools and operational 
data. 
 
Tool use is especially important in agentic AI systems. Instead of depending 
only on the knowledge stored inside the model, the agent can call external 
tools such as APIs, databases, search systems, calculators, or software 
services. Qu et al. (2025) describe tool learning as a process where LLMs

In the context of eMAS, tool use allows the Factory Agent to connect natural-

2.3.5 Retrieval-Augmented Generation in Industrial Knowledge Support 
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is an approach that improves LLM 
responses by retrieving relevant external information before generating an 
answer. This is useful because LLMs may have outdated knowledge, may not 
know private domain-specific information, or may generate unsupported 
answers. Klesel & Wittmann (2025) explain that RAG can support information 
access by combining conversational AI with external knowledge sources, 
allowing users to retrieve more relevant information through natural-language 
queries. 
RAG is important in complex information environments because knowledge 
may be stored across many documents, manuals, records, or databases. In 
manufacturing, useful information may exist in maintenance procedures, 
scheduling rules, safety documents, operation manuals, and internal reports. 
A normal LLM may not have access to this information unless it is provided 
during the interaction. RAG helps solve this problem by retrieving relevant 
context from the available knowledge base before producing the final 
response. 


--- PAGE 33 ---
 
21 
 
For eMAS, RAG supports document-based advisory responses. When a user 
asks a knowledge-related question, the AI assistant can retrieve relevant 
information from available documents and use it to prepare a more grounded 
answer. This is useful for factory-management tasks where users may need 
explanations, rules, or references instead of only database values. RAG also 
improves trust because the answer can be linked to retrieved knowledge 
sources, allowing the user to verify the information. Therefore, RAG 
strengthens eMAS as a decision-support system by connecting natural-
language interaction with operational and document-based knowledge. 
 
2.3.6  Human-in-the-Loop Approval for Safe AI Automation 
Human-in-the-Loop (HITL) design refers to systems where humans remain 
involved in important stages of AI decision-making or action execution. This is 
especially important when AI systems are used in high-impact or operational 
environments. Chen et al. (2023) explain that HITL combines human 
intelligence and machine intelligence to support more ethical and controlled AI 
systems. Instead of allowing AI to act fully on its own, HITL keeps human 
judgment involved where accuracy, responsibility, or safety is important. 
In manufacturing management, fully automated AI actions can create risk if 
the system changes schedules, updates records, or applies operational 
decisions without human review. Even if the AI assistant can identify a useful 
action, the final decision should still be checked by a responsible user when 
the action affects business data. This is because manufacturing records are 
connected to production planning, inventory, machine allocation, and reporting. 
A wrong change may affect other parts of the workflow. 
For this reason, eMAS separates read-only AI assistance from business-
changing actions. Read-only tasks, such as retrieving reports or checking 
machine status, can be completed directly. However, actions that modify 
operational records must go through human approval before execution. This 
approval workflow improves traceability and helps prevent unintended 
changes. It also supports user trust because the AI assistant becomes a 


--- PAGE 34 ---
 
22 
 
controlled decision-support tool rather than an uncontrolled automation 
system. Therefore, HITL approval is a critical part of the eMAS safety design.

interoperability and tool registry design therefore support the current eMAS 
architecture by connecting natural-language interaction with real operational

2.5 Research Gap 
The literature review shows that Industry 4.0 technologies, multi-agent 
systems, natural-language processing, and AI-based decision support 
systems have each contributed to modern manufacturing management. 
However, many existing systems address these areas separately. Advanced 
planning and scheduling systems are strong in optimization and production 
planning, but they often require structured input, technical knowledge, and 
manual configuration. On the other hand, chatbot-based systems may 
improve user interaction, but they are often limited to answering questions and 
may not be deeply connected to operational factory data or safe workflow 
execution. 
 
This creates a gap between intelligent conversation and practical 
manufacturing management. Factory users need systems that can not only 
understand natural-language requests, but also retrieve accurate operational 


--- PAGE 44 ---
 
32 
 
data, support scheduling and reporting tasks, explain recommendations, and 
prevent unsafe automated changes. In many existing tools, natural-language 
interaction, backend operational integration, approval control, and decision 
support are not combined into one lightweight prototype. 
 
This research addresses the gap by developing eMAS as an AI-assisted 
engineering management prototype. eMAS connects a natural-language 
Factory Agent with backend manufacturing APIs, simulated factory data, 
scheduling and reporting functions, and approval-controlled actions. The 
research therefore explores how an agentic system can support practical 
manufacturing workflows while still keeping human users involved in important 
business-changing decisions.

3.2 Artificial Intelligence Agent Theory and the BDI Model 
AI Agent Theory defines an agent as an autonomous entity capable of 
perceiving its environment, reasoning about its current state, and performing 
actions to achieve defined goals (Russell, Stuart & Norvig, 2021). Unlike 
traditional software systems that rely on fixed and predefined procedures, 
intelligent agents operate autonomously and are able to adapt their behavior 
in response to environmental changes. In this research, the agents within the 
Engineering Management AI Agentic System (eMAS) are designed based on 
the Belief–Desire–Intention (BDI) model, which provides a structured cognitive 
framework for agent reasoning. Within this model, beliefs represent the 
agent’s understanding of the factory environment, including machine status, 
production schedules, resource availability, and detected disruptions, while 
desires define the operational objectives the agent seeks to achieve, such as 
minimizing production delays, reducing operational errors, and improving 
workflow efficiency. Intentions correspond to the specific actions selected by 
the agent, including rescheduling tasks, generating alerts, or executing 
automated operations. By applying the BDI model, eMAS agents are able to 
dynamically revise their intentions when new information updates their beliefs, 
enabling adaptive and context-aware behavior. This theoretical application 
directly supports the research objective of evaluating whether AI agents can 
reduce daily operational errors and accelerate engineering management tasks.

it can be used to affect reasoning and decision-making. 
 
This translation is once more conceptualized as a multistage process in eMAS. 
The intent recognition is first performed to detect the communicative intention

parameters are mapped to symbolic or structured beliefs change, altering the 
internal agent state description of the factory state. This theoretical connection 
can be linked with the theory of BDI agents, where belief changes cause the 
thinking processes that can update desires and create new intentions. 
 
The current developments in large language models (LLMs) also contribute to 
this paradigm of interaction by providing semantic parsing and context-
sensitive interpretation of user input, and more flexible and robust mapping 
between natural language expressions and the belief structures of agents. 
Instead of acting like a black box, NLP in eMAS is then theorized to be an

3.4 Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS) and Smart Factory Context 
This study based mainly on the Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS) theory and 
interoperability theory. CPS theory describes how physical processes and 
computational intelligence are brought together using constant feedback so 
that the systems can watch, understand and adjust their behavior in real time 
even when in a complex environment. 
 
The factory is designed as a system of physical-digital interaction where the 
physical layer is the set of machines, sensors, and other resources involved 
in production, and intelligent agents are the participants of the cyber layer. 
Physical environmental changes are felt and measured in the form of digital 
state updates and create a closed interaction between physical and 
computational space. 
 
The interoperability theory is a complement of CPS theory, as it explains how 
the heterogeneous systems and agents can exchange information in a 
consistent and meaningful manner. Instead of specialized protocols or 
implementations, interoperability theory puts emphasis on common 
information models, semantic alignment, and standardized interfaces as a 
requirement to coordinate reasoning between distributed systems. This is the 
theoretical perspective that underlies the supposition that the agents are able 
to have a consistent and updated model of the factory state with heterogeneity 
in the system. The process is equivalent to CPS theory, which is an adaptive 
control loop of sensing, computation, and action.

account of how the smart factory environment enables adaptive and agent-
driven reasoning. This theoretical framework underpins the legitimacy of the 
application of concepts as a component of the eMAS structure and justifies 
the assessment of the system responsiveness and flexibility in the changing 
manufacturing conditions. 
 
3.5 Multi-Agent System Concepts and Agent Coordination 
The Multi-Agent System (MAS) theory involves research into the systems that 
consist of multiple autonomous agents interacting and coordinating to resolve 
problems that are challenging or inefficient to solve by centralized control. 
MAS theory is especially applicable in manufacturing situations because 
production resources are distributed, production processes are parallel, and 
local disruptions are frequent. 
 
The agents in eMAS are designed with specific functions, including monitoring 
agent, scheduling agent and decision-support agent, whose functions are 
based on the BDI cognitive model. Although in autonomy, all the agents 
respond to local events independently, it is the system-level effectiveness that 
requires coordination mechanisms. 
 
Negotiation-based coordination models, including the Contract Net Protocol 
(CNP), are theoretically based on the coordination of agents in eMAS. In this 
context, an agent that perceives a disturbance (e.g., a machine failure) may 
take the role of a manager agent and post a task announcement to other 
agents. Resource agents or scheduling agents can then consider their local 
beliefs and abilities and make bids. Depending on preset standards, including 
a low delay or minimal resource use, the manager agent chooses the most 
appropriate proposal and allocates the task. 


--- PAGE 50 ---
 
38 
 
 
This coordination theory makes sure that the interaction between agents is not 
by chance but it is guided by clear interaction regulations balancing the local 
autonomy against global optimization. The idea that eMAS is better suited to 
dynamic conditions of production as compared to the traditional centralized or 
rule-based manufacturing management systems is reinforced by application 
of MAS coordination theory. 
 
3.6 Decision Support System (DSS) Theory 
The concept of the Decision Support System (DSS) is directed at information 
systems oriented at supporting human decision-makers with the help of the 
provision of relevant information, analysis tools, and recommendations. There 
are three broad categories of DSS namely passive, active and cooperative 
systems. Passive DSS are those which simply present data without giving 
direct recommendations whereas active DSS are those which automatically 
give suggestions or decisions based on the analysis of the system. 
 
In this categorization, eMAS is theoretically placed as an Active Decision 
Support System. Instead of showing dashboards or historical reports, eMAS 
agents also constantly examine real-time factory information, make judgments 
about the present and future system states, and makes suggestions in 
advance, including rescheduling operations, priority changes, or operational 
warnings. 
 
It is the embedding of AI agent reasoning in the DSS framework which means 
that the engineering managers do not have to carry the cognitive load of 
routine analysis and exception handling because it is entrusted to autonomous 
agents. Human users are also in the loop of decision-making but are facilitated 
with timely and contextual insights on the system.

This section explicitly presents eMAS as an Active DSS, which allows 
distinguishing between the traditional manufacturing information system and 
offers a clearer theoretical framework on how to assess the increase in the 
level of decision accuracy, planning speed, and managerial efficiency.

NLP provides the human-agent interface by allowing users to express factory-

In the implemented prototype, these theories are represented through a

Chapter 4 Research Methodology  
4.1 Introduction  
This chapter describes the research methodology used to develop and 
evaluate the Engineering Management AI Agentic System (eMAS). Since the 
project has progressed from an interim prototype into a working full-stack 
system, the methodology focuses on both system development and system 
evaluation. The evaluation is designed to measure whether eMAS can support 
manufacturing management tasks, reduce manual work, provide useful 
decision support, and maintain safe AI-assisted operations. 
 
The current eMAS system consists of a React-based frontend, a Go backend 
API, a MySQL database, and a Python FastAPI Factory Agent service. The

and resource management, inventory, products and BOM, scheduling, 
shortage resolution, production data, reports, dashboards, and an AI assistant. 
Therefore, the research methodology must evaluate not only the usability of 
the interface, but also the correctness, reliability, safety, and integration of 
these system components. 
 
Due to limited access to a live industrial factory and confidential manufacturing 
data, the evaluation is conducted using seeded manufacturing data, simulated 
factory scenarios, automated tests, system logs, API responses, Factory 
Agent traces, and limited proxy-user testing where available. This approach 
allows eMAS to be evaluated in a controlled and repeatable environment while 
still reflecting realistic factory-management workflows.

4.3.2 System Architecture Design 
After the system requirements were identified, the overall architecture of 
eMAS was designed as a layered full-stack system. The architecture 
separates the user interface, backend operational services, data storage, and 
AI agent components so that each part of the system can be developed, tested, 
and improved independently. This design allows eMAS to support normal 
manufacturing management functions while also providing natural-language 
AI assistance. 
 
The frontend layer is developed using React and Vite. It provides the main 
user interface for dashboards, job scheduling, production data, reports, 
inventory, machine resources, shortage resolution, and the AI assistant. Users 
can interact with the system either through normal menu-based pages or 
through the AI assistant using natural-language requests. 
 
The backend operational layer is implemented using Go with the Gin 
framework. It provides REST API endpoints for core manufacturing functions 
such as jobs, machines, inventory, production logs, quality records, reports, 
scheduling, and approvals. The backend communicates with a MySQL 
database, which stores structured factory data used by both the frontend and 


--- PAGE 60 ---
 
48 
 
the AI assistant. This layer acts as the main source of operational truth for the 
system. 
 
The AI layer is implemented as a separate Python FastAPI Factory Agent 
service. This service receives natural-language requests from the frontend 
and processes them through an agent workflow. The Factory Agent identifies 
the user’s intent, selects suitable backend tools, retrieves relevant operational 
data, and prepares responses for the user. When the request requires 
document-based knowledge, retrieval-augmented generation is used to 
retrieve relevant information before generating an answer. 
 
For safety, eMAS separates read-only actions from business-changing actions. 
If the AI assistant needs to perform an action that may modify operational 
records, the system prepares the action for human review before execution. 
The user must approve the action before it is applied. Server-Sent Events are 
used to send live progress updates from the Factory Agent back to the 
frontend, allowing the user to follow the status of the AI workflow. 
 
Overall, the architecture supports a modular and safe AI-assisted 
manufacturing management system. The frontend handles interaction, the Go 
backend manages factory operations and data, the database stores structured 
records, and the Factory Agent provides natural-language decision support 
and workflow automation.

Evidence is collected from approval workflow tests, frontend approval behavior, 
Factory Agent session traces, and backend transaction tests. 
 
This evaluation is important because eMAS uses AI to support factory 
operations. Without approval control, AI automation may create safety and 
accountability risks. The approval workflow ensures that the user remains 
responsible for final business decisions while still benefiting from AI assistance.

4.6 Data Analysis Techniques  
The collected data will be analyzed using both quantitative and qualitative 
techniques. Quantitative analysis will be used to measure system 
performance, task efficiency, automation benefits, and reliability. The main 
quantitative measures include task completion time, number of user 
interactions, response time, successful task completion rate, scheduling 
correctness, report or query accuracy, approval workflow completion, and 
automated test pass rate. Descriptive statistics such as mean, minimum, 
maximum, standard deviation, and percentage improvement will be calculated 
to summarize these results. 
 
Where suitable, comparative analysis will be performed between normal 
menu-based workflows and AI-assisted workflows. For example, the number 
of steps required to find a report or check scheduling information can be 
compared with the number of steps required when using the AI assistant. If 
enough user or proxy-user task data is collected, statistical tests such as 
paired sample t-tests or non-parametric alternatives may be used to check 
whether the observed differences are meaningful. However, if the sample size 
is small, the analysis will focus more on descriptive comparison and practical 
improvement rather than strong statistical claims. 
 
Qualitative analysis will be used to interpret user feedback, observations, and 
comments about the system. The feedback will be grouped into themes such

4.7 Reliability and Validity 
Reliability is supported through repeatable evaluation procedures and

scenarios, backend API tests, frontend component tests, Factory Agent tests,

executed repeatedly and help confirm whether eMAS behaves consistently 
under controlled conditions. 
 
End-to-end testing improves reliability because it checks the full workflow

appear in isolated unit tests. 
 
Validity is supported by aligning the evaluation measures with the research 
objectives. Since eMAS aims to improve factory-management access, reduce 
manual work, support operational decision-making, and maintain safe AI-

test scheduling, shortage detection, inventory checking, and production 
planning in a controlled way. 
 
However, the system is still limited by the use of simulated data. Although the 
dataset is representative, it does not fully capture the uncertainty and 
complexity of a real factory environment, such as unexpected machine 
breakdowns, supplier delays, operator constraints, urgent customer changes, 
and incomplete production records. 
 
6.3 Discussion of Scheduling and Shortage Resolution 
The scheduling and shortage-resolution results are one of the most important 
parts of the project because they show how eMAS supports decision-making 
instead of only displaying data. The scheduling function evaluates jobs against 
machines, process steps, material requirements, BOM dependencies, and 
expected arrival dates. The shortage-resolution function helps users 
understand what materials or planned products are needed to resolve 
production constraints. 
 
The evaluation showed that the system can handle important scheduling 
correctness checks, including machine overlap validation, invalid time range 
prevention, missing slot detection, duplicate slot prevention, step-order 
validation, and material-shortage evidence checking. The recorded proposal 


--- PAGE 89 ---
 
77 
 
overlap verification showed 89 proposal slots with 0 overlaps, which supports 
the reliability of the scheduling output in the tested seeded scenario. 
 
The shortage-resolution workflow is also useful because it provides a clearer 
explanation of material problems. Instead of only showing that a job is 
infeasible, the system can show shortage rows and affected jobs. This 
improves transparency and helps users understand what action is required. 
The survey result also supports this finding, as shortage-resolution clarity 
received a high mean score of 4.90 out of 5.00. 
 
However, shortage resolution must remain explainable. If too many jobs are 
marked infeasible without clear root-cause evidence, users may lose 
confidence in the system. Therefore, the system should continue to improve 
how it separates true material shortages, delayed material availability, 
resource overload, and subproduct dependency issues. 
 
6.4 Discussion of Factory Agent and AI-Assisted Interaction 
The Factory Agent improves eMAS by allowing users to interact with factory 
data using natural language. This is useful because factory-management 
systems often require users to search through many pages manually. With the 
AI assistant, users can ask questions about jobs, inventory, shortages, 
schedules, reports, and factory status. 
The evaluation results show that the Factory Agent is supported by strong 
automated testing. The Factory Agent Python test suite recorded 1319 passed 
tests with 0 failed tests, while the Factory Agent node benchmark suite 
recorded 87 passed tests with 0 failed tests. The frontend/backend oracle 
contract suite also recorded 185 passed tests with 0 failed tests. The system 
generated 138 API tools from the backend Swagger definition, which supports 
alignment between the AI assistant and backend API functions.

The survey result also supports the usefulness of the AI assistant. The 
statement “The AI assistant understands factory-related questions” achieved 
a mean score of 4.80, while “The AI assistant provides useful information for 
decision-making” achieved a mean score of 4.50. This indicates that 
participants generally found the AI assistant useful. 
However, the AI assistant should not be treated as fully autonomous. Its role 
is to assist users by retrieving data, explaining results, and supporting 
decisions. Important operational actions should still require human approval, 
especially when changing schedules, jobs, or business records. 
 
6.5 Discussion of Safety and Approval Control 
The approval workflow is an important safety feature in eMAS. Since the 
system includes an AI assistant that can support action-based workflows, it is 
necessary to prevent uncontrolled changes to operational data. The approval 
mechanism ensures that data-changing actions are reviewed by the user 
before being applied. 
 
The evaluation showed that approval-related workflows passed through 
automated tests and E2E verification. The survey also showed positive 
feedback, with the approval workflow safety statement receiving a mean score 
of 4.50. This suggests that users felt safer when the system required approval 
before data-changing actions. 
 
This design is suitable for manufacturing management because incorrect 
schedule changes, job updates, or inventory actions may affect production 
planning. By keeping the human user in control, eMAS supports AI-assisted 
decision-making without removing user responsibility.

6.6 Discussion of Reliability and Testing 
The automated testing results provide strong evidence for system reliability. 
Across the non-overlapping final test groups, 1713 counted tests passed with 
0 failures. This includes 197 frontend unit/component tests, 62 mocked 
Playwright E2E tests, 48 seeded full-stack oracle E2E tests, 1319 Factory 
Agent Python tests, and 87 Factory Agent node benchmark tests. Additional 
focused verification gates also passed, including 35 response-document E2E 
tests and 185 frontend/backend oracle contract tests. These focused gates 
are reported separately because they overlap with broader test suites.The 100% 
pass rate is important because it shows that the executed final verification 
gates passed successfully. However, this result should be interpreted correctly. 
It means that the selected final test gates passed with 0 failures, not that every 
possible future scenario or optional environment-dependent test has been 
covered. 
The use of E2E testing is especially valuable for this project because eMAS is 
an integrated system. A complete workflow may involve the frontend, backend 
API, database, Factory Agent, scheduling logic, server-sent event updates, 
response-document rendering, and approval workflow. Therefore, E2E testing 
provides stronger evidence than only testing individual functions separately. 
 
6.7 Discussion of User Survey Results 
The user survey results show positive early feedback on the usability and 
usefulness of eMAS. The survey involved 10 participants and achieved an 
overall mean score of 4.71 out of 5.00. Out of 100 total ratings, 99 ratings were 
either Agree or Strongly Agree, giving an overall positive response rate of 99%. 
 
The strongest results were interface navigation and overall usefulness, both 
with mean scores of 5.00. This suggests that participants found the system 
easy to navigate and useful for factory-management support. Shortage-

The lowest score was job and inventory access, with a mean score of 4.40. 
Although this is still positive, it shows that job and inventory navigation can be 
improved. Future improvements may include better filters, clearer linking 
between jobs and materials, and faster access from shortage results to related 
inventory records. 
 
The survey supports the automated evaluation findings, but it also has 
limitations. The sample size was small, and the participants were not actual 
factory operators using live production data. Therefore, the survey should be 
treated as early usability feedback rather than a complete industrial usability 
study. 
 
6.8 Limitations of the Current Evaluation 
Although the evaluation results are positive, several limitations remain. First, 
the system was evaluated using simulated factory data rather than live factory 
data. This means that the system has not yet been tested against real 
operational uncertainty, real supplier delays, real machine downtime, or real 
production pressure. 
 
Second, the survey involved only 10 participants. This is suitable for prototype 
feedback, but it is not enough to represent all factory users. A larger study with 
real factory managers, planners, operators, and inventory staff would provide 
stronger usability evidence. 
 
Third, the system has not yet been deployed in a real production environment. 
Deployment would introduce additional concerns such as user roles, data

Finally, the AI assistant depends on the quality of available data and tool

remain important for future improvement. 
 
6.9 Summary 
In summary, the discussion shows that eMAS has achieved strong prototype-

AI-assisted interaction, scheduling support, shortage resolution, approval-

positive user feedback. 
 
However, the system should still be improved and validated in a real factory 
environment. The most important future needs are live-data testing, larger 
user evaluation, stronger job and inventory navigation, production deployment 
validation, and continued improvement of AI explanation quality.

7.2 Achievement of Project Objectives 
The first objective of the project was to design and develop an integrated 
manufacturing management system. This objective was achieved through the 
development of multiple connected modules, including dashboard, jobs, 
machines, inventory, products and BOM, scheduling, shortage resolution, 
reports, predictive analysis, and settings. These modules allow factory-related 
information to be managed in one system instead of being separated across 
disconnected tools. 
 
The second objective was to support production scheduling and shortage-
resolution decision-making. This objective was achieved through scheduling 
proposal generation, machine allocation validation, BOM-based material 
checking, expected arrival handling, and shortage-resolution rows. The 
scheduling evaluation showed 89 proposal slots with 0 overlaps, while the 
shortage-resolution workflow helped explain material and subproduct-related 
constraints. 
 
The third objective was to provide AI-assisted interaction for factory-
management tasks. This objective was achieved through the Factory Agent, 
which allows users to ask factory-related questions, retrieve operational data, 
receive explanations, and interact with system workflows using natural 
language. The Factory Agent was supported by 138 generated backend API 
tools and passed the recorded automated verification gates. 
 
The fourth objective was to improve safety for AI-supported actions. This 
objective was achieved through the approval workflow. The system requires 
user approval before applying data-changing actions, which helps prevent 
unsafe automatic changes. This is important in manufacturing because 
incorrect operational changes may affect jobs, schedules, inventory, and 
production planning.

7.3 Conclusion 
In conclusion, eMAS has successfully developed into a working AI-assisted 
manufacturing management prototype. The system is able to support factory-
management activities such as job monitoring, inventory checking, production 
scheduling, shortage resolution, report access, AI-assisted querying, and 
approval-based control. 
The evaluation results show that the system performs well under simulated 
manufacturing conditions. The seeded dataset provided representative 
manufacturing records, while the automated tests provided strong technical 
evidence of reliability. The final non-overlapping counted test groups recorded 
1713 passed tests with 0 failures. Additional focused verification gates, 
including response-document E2E and frontend/backend oracle contract tests, 
also passed and provided extra evidence for important workflows. The user 
survey also showed positive feedback, with an overall mean score of 4.71 out 
of 5.00 and a 99% positive response rate. 
Overall, the project demonstrates that AI-assisted factory-management 
systems can help reduce manual checking work, improve access to 
operational data, support scheduling decisions, and improve transparency in

should be further validated using real factory data and real industrial users. 
7.4 Recommendations 
Several recommendations are proposed for future improvement. First, eMAS 
should be tested with real factory data. The current evaluation used simulated 
data, which is suitable for prototype testing, but real production data would 
provide stronger evidence of practical usefulness. Future testing should 
include real jobs, machines, inventory records, supplier arrivals, production 
logs, and downtime events. 
Second, the system should be evaluated by real factory users. The current 
survey involved 10 participants and provided useful early feedback, but future 
evaluation should involve factory managers, production planners, operators, 
and inventory staff. This would help identify real workflow problems that may 
not appear during prototype testing. 
 
Third, job and inventory navigation should be improved. The survey showed 
that job and inventory access had the lowest mean score, although it was still 
positive. Future improvements may include advanced search, clearer filters, 
direct links between jobs and related materials, and better navigation from 
shortage rows to inventory records. 
 
Fourth, the shortage-resolution explanation should continue to be improved. 
Shortage results must remain clear and traceable so that users understand 
why a job is affected and what action is required. Future improvements may 
include clearer root-cause labels, shortage priority ranking, and visual 
explanation of material flow from BOM to affected jobs. 
 
Fifth, production deployment readiness should be strengthened. Before real 
factory deployment, the system should include stronger role-based access 
control, backup and recovery procedures, security testing, audit logs, 


--- PAGE 97 ---
 
85 
 
monitoring, and deployment documentation. These are important because 
factory systems may contain sensitive operational and business data. 
 
Sixth, the AI assistant should continue to improve its explanation and decision-
support ability. Future work can improve the assistant’s ability to explain 
scheduling constraints, compare alternative actions, summarise shortage 
causes, and provide more transparent evidence for recommendations. 
 
7.5 Future Work 
Future work should focus on moving eMAS from a prototype into a more 
production-ready system. The most important future work is real factory 
integration. This may include connecting eMAS with existing ERP, MES, 
inventory, or machine-monitoring systems so that the platform can use live 
operational data. 
 
Another future improvement is advanced analytics. The predictive analysis 
module can be expanded to include machine failure prediction, production 
delay prediction, material demand forecasting, and capacity planning. These 
features would make the system more useful for long-term factory decision-
making. 
 
The scheduling function can also be improved by adding more optimisation 
objectives. For example, future scheduling can consider delivery priority, 
machine setup time, operator availability, energy usage, maintenance 
windows, and cost. This would allow the scheduler to provide more realistic 
production plans. 
 
The user interface can also be improved based on survey feedback. Better job 
and inventory navigation, clearer shortage displays, and more visual 


--- PAGE 98 ---
 
86 
 
scheduling explanations would improve user confidence and reduce learning 
effort. 
 
Finally, future work should include a larger user study. A larger evaluation with 
real factory users would provide stronger evidence of usability, usefulness, 
and decision-support impact. The study could measure task completion time, 
number of user interactions, error rate, satisfaction score, and comparison 
with existing manual processes. 
 
7.6 Final Summary 
The eMAS project achieved its main aim of developing an AI-assisted 
manufacturing management system prototype. The system integrates factory 
data, supports scheduling and shortage-resolution workflows, provides AI-
assisted interaction, and includes approval-based safety control. The 
evaluation results show strong technical reliability and positive user feedback. 
 
Although further real-world validation is required, the project demonstrates 
that eMAS has practical potential for improving factory-management support. 
With future improvements in live-data integration, real-user evaluation, 
deployment readiness, and advanced decision support, eMAS can be further 
developed into a more complete manufacturing assistance platform.
